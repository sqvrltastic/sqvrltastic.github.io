Full guide to RSS

What is RSS?

From Wikipedia:

"RSS (which stands for "Really Simple Syndication") is a web feed that allows users and applications to access updates to websites in a standardized, computer-readable format."

In layman terms, basically, RSS is a website's news feed that is standardized and which makes it possible for you to "subscribe" to your favorite website's news feed without actually creating an account there or signing up with your email for a newsletter or anything like that.

On its own, RSS feed appears as a file in an XML format with a weird looking code inside that looks similar to HTML. You will need an RSS feed reader program to actually convert that code to a human-readable feed.

There are a lot of RSS readers out there, both for PC and mobile, personally I use "Fluent Reader" on my Windows PC; you can get it from Microsoft Store. It's completely free and the code is open source. On mobile, I use "Feeder".

Why use RSS?

Because social media feeds suck. You always get irrelevant, garbage posts, ads and you often don't even see the posts from people that you follow because of the algorithm. Not to mention that, often you can get distracted and you have to manually check each website individually. With RSS, you have all of your subscriptions in one place.

You can subscribe to news sites, blogs, personal websites (if they have an RSS feed implemented) and also Tumblr blogs, subreddits, AO3, Bluesky profile feeds and even YouTube channels and many more! Though, take note that not every website takes its RSS feed seriously (shame!) and some of them are a bit poorly implemented.

Also, RSS is much better in terms of privacy and security because you don't need to give anyone your data, like in the case of signing up to an email newsletter with your email.

For users: How to use RSS

As I said already, you will need an RSS feed reader program. On PC, you can use Fluent Reader like I do, or choose whichever one you like the most. They are mostly similar.

On mobile, as previously mentioned, I use Feeder, but you can just search for "RSS reader" in whichever app store you have installed on your phone. Also, there is the F-Droid app store that has only FOSS apps there, meaning, those are usually apps that don't track you and don't show you ads or beg you to buy in-app purchases. I personally use it and can fully recommend it.

First, go to the website you want to subscribe to. Look for the RSS logo that usually looks like a Wi-Fi symbol over an orange background (). Click on it; it should take you to the website's RSS feed. Simply copy the URL, launch your RSS feed reader, look for a button that says something along the lines of "add new source" and paste it there.

If you can't find the RSS icon, don't fret. Try looking for an RSS feed by accessing it directly through your browser's address bar. Usually, the website's RSS feed is located at https://www.website.com/rss.xml, or at https://www.website.com/index.xml, or at https://www.website.com/rss, but it can vary. As an example, this website's RSS is located here.

Some social media have a hidden RSS feed that you can access; some examples:

Tumblr: https://username.tumblr.com/rss

Bluesky: https://bsky.app/profile/username/rss

Reddit: https://www.reddit.com/r/subredditname.rss or https://www.reddit.com/u/username.rss

YouTube: Requires a bit of code digging, unfortunately. On PC:

(show steps)
  1. Open the YouTube channel in your browser.
  2. Right-click "view page source".
  3. Press Ctrl + F on your keyboard.
  4. Search for "browse_id" (without the quotation marks).
  5. You should find a part of the code that looks like this: {"key":"browse_id","value":"UC_x5XG1OV2P6uZZ5FSM9Ttw"}, copy the string of random letters and numbers that is inside the "value" field, that is the channel's ID.
  6. Create a working URL to YouTube's channel RSS feed by replacing the "CHANNELID" part inside this URL template: https://www.youtube.com/feeds/videos.xml?channel_id=CHANNELID with the ID you just copied.

Alternatively, on Android, just use PipePipe (my current fav), NewPipe or whatever other software fork there is.

Feel free to look up online if a website has an RSS feed. A lot of them do, but they can be hidden.

For developers: How to add an RSS feed to your website

Now that I have hopefully convinced you of how great RSS is, here is a tutorial on how to actually implement it and add it to your website.

Some website builders or SSGs, like for example Hugo which I use, have an option to auto generate RSS feeds, but I personally prefer not to use it and instead I prefer to manually write updates to my RSS feed, at least for this website. That way I feel I have more control.

First, create a rss.xml file in the root directly of your website (meaning, the top-most folder where all of your website's content resides). Then, copy and paste the code below. Keep the overall structure and replace the content of each field with your own where applicable.

Base of your RSS feed (show code)
<?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?>
<rss version="2.0" xmlns:atom="http://www.w3.org/2005/Atom">
    <channel>
        <title>Title of your website</title>
        <description>
            Description of your website
        </description>
        <link>https://www.yourwebsite.com/rss.xml</link>
        <atom:link
            href="https://www.yourwebsite.com/rss.xml"
            rel="self"
            type="application/rss+xml"
        />
        <ttl>1440</ttl>
        <webMaster>youremail@example.com</webMaster>
        <lastBuildDate>
            Time and date when the RSS feed was last updated in RFC2822 format
        </lastBuildDate>
        <language>en-us</language>

  <!--
  Place your feed items here.
  This is just a comment,
  you can delete it later.
  -->

    </channel>
</rss>

Next, you will want to add individual items or entries to your RSS feed. Below are code snippets that you can copy and paste.

Basic feed item without any links (show code)

I use this one when I just want to notify my subscribers of some small general update, for example about improving some parts of a site's functionality. It doesn't link to any larger page. Think of it as a log entry.

<item>
    <title>Title of your post</title>
    <description>
        Description of your post
    </description>
    <pubDate>
        Time and date when the item was made in RFC2822 format
    </pubDate>
</item>
Feed item that links to a full blog post (show code)

This is probably the most common way people structure RSS items. This method is most suitable for when you have, for example, a recipe or a blog post that is mostly text based.

Basically what it does is, it displays a title and a description (which acts as a preview to your full post) and when you click on "view full" in your RSS reader, it should switch to the blog post itself.

<item>
    <guid>https://www.yourwebsite.com/link-to-your-post.html</guid>
    <title>Title of your post</title>
    <description>
        Description of your post
    </description>
    <link>https://www.yourwebsite.com/link-to-your-post.html</link>
    <pubDate>
        Time and date when the item was made in RFC2822 format
    </pubDate>
</item>
Feed item with an image and a complex description that links to a site page (show code)

A more complex version of the previous example that can be used to showcase artwork or photos. It also demonstrates how you can use some basic HTML tags inside the <description> field to add some optional extra functionality.

Do note that <a> tags (links) tend to bunch up at the end of the paragraph all the time (at least for me, in Fluent Reader), even if they are placed in the middle of it. I have no idea why.

<item>
    <guid>https://www.yourwebsite.com/link-to-your-post.html</guid>
    <title>Title of your post</title>
    <description>
        <img src="https://www.yourwebsite.com/images/link-to-your-image.jpg" />
        <p>
            This is the first paragraph of a description and it contains a
            <a href="https://www.anywebsite.com/any-link.html">link</a>
        </p>
        <p>
            This is the second paragraph
        </p>
    </description>
    <enclosure 
        url="https://www.yourwebsite.com/images/link-to-your-image.jpg" 
        type="image/jpeg"
        length="image file size in bytes"
    />
    <link>https://www.yourwebsite.com/link-to-your-post.html</link>
    <pubDate>
        Time and date when the item was made in RFC2822 format
    </pubDate>
</item>

Detailed explanation of each RSS code element

<ttl> — "time to live"; tells your RSS reader how often to refresh the page. Time is set in minutes. Putting 1440 as value means that your RSS feed refreshes once a day, which is enough if you don't update your website all that often.

<webMaster> — optional; your email for contact.

<lastBuildDate> — timestamp that shows the last time the RSS feed was updated, in RFC2822 format. You can generate an RFC 2822 timestamp on sites like the UNIX timestamp generator. It looks something like this when generated: Sat, 28 Dec 2024 16:26:14 +0000.

<pubDate> — timestamp that shows when the individual item was published, in RFC2822 format. Similar to <lastBuildDate>.

<guid> — (from rssboard.org):

"<guid> is an optional sub-element of <item>. guid stands for globally unique identifier. It's a string that uniquely identifies the item. When present, an aggregator may choose to use this string to determine if an item is new."

Usually, its content is the same as the <link> tag. Also, keep in mind that you have to use absolute (full path) and not relative links!

Also, another important note, with <guid> and <link>, don't do this:

(show code)
<!-- Don't do this!-->
<item>
    <guid>
        https://www.yourwebsite.com/link-to-your-post.html
    </guid>
    <title>Title of your post</title>
    <description>
        Description of your post
    </description>
    <link>
        https://www.yourwebsite.com/link-to-your-post.html
    </link>
    <pubDate>
        Time and date when the item was made in RFC2822 format
    </pubDate>
</item>

Don't transfer the hyperlink itself onto the new line and then indent it. I found that formatting it like that creates spaces before the link so the link becomes invalid!

<enclosure> — supposedly optional, according to rssboard.org; a sub element of <item> that allows for embedding media; in my case, images.

I did some tests and found out that on some RSS readers, using only the <enclosure> element is not enough and it doesn't embed the image, but on others it does. It seems a bit inconsistent. That is why I always use the <img> tag inside the <description> element because I found that to be the most foolproof way to add images to RSS items, but I usually prefer to add <enclosure> element as well because it doesn't hurt I suppose.

Some more info about the <enclosure> element...

<enclosure> has three attributes: url, length and type. url is a link that points to your media file's location, length is the size of the file in bytes and type specifies the media type (MIME type).

Note that, while MIME type and file format are not exactly the same thing, in practice they often closely align. For example, if you have a .jpg image, the MIME type would be "image/jpeg".

To get the length of a file (in my case, image), go to a folder where the image is located, open a bash terminal in that folder and enter a command: stat --format=%s your-image-name.jpg. Copy the string of numbers you've got.

Since I'm on Windows I use Git Bash and I will assume that you know at least remotely how to use it. There are other ways to do this, but I found this one to be the simplest one.

Now, hard part is over, now you just need to combine the two and fill your RSS feed with posts... Below is an example of how that might look like.

Combining it all together (show code)
<?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?>
    <rss version="2.0" xmlns:atom="http://www.w3.org/2005/Atom">
        <channel>
            <title>Example Website</title>
            <description>
                This is an example website to showcase RSS feeds
            </description>
            <link>https://www.examplewebsite.com/rss.xml</link>
            <atom:link
                href="https://www.examplewebsite.com/rss.xml"
                rel="self"
                type="application/rss+xml"
            />
            <ttl>1440</ttl>
            <webMaster>email@example.com</webMaster>
            <lastBuildDate>
                Sat, 28 Dec 2024 20:55:43 +0000
            </lastBuildDate>
            <language>en-us</language>
    
            <item>
                <guid>https://www.examplewebsite.com/post3.html</guid>
                <title>Post 3</title>
                <description>
                    This is the 3rd post
                </description>
                <link>https://www.examplewebsite.com/post3.html</link>
                <pubDate>
                    Sat, 28 Dec 2024 20:55:43 +0000
                </pubDate>
            </item>

            <item>
                <guid>https://www.examplewebsite.com/post2.html</guid>
                <title>Post 2</title>
                <description>
                    This is the 2nd post
                </description>
                <link>https://www.examplewebsite.com/post2.html</link>
                <pubDate>
                    Sat, 28 Dec 2024 19:50:43 +0000
                </pubDate>
            </item>

            <item>
                <guid>https://www.examplewebsite.com/post1.html</guid>
                <title>Post 1</title>
                <description>
                    This is the 1st post
                </description>
                <link>https://www.examplewebsite.com/post1.html</link>
                <pubDate>
                    Sat, 28 Dec 2024 18:49:43 +0000
                </pubDate>
            </item>
    
        </channel>
    </rss>

Tip: if you want to include some special characters in your RSS feed, like "&" for example, make sure to write them as a HTML entity, so as "&#x26;" (without the quotation marks) in this case. Here is a basic HTML entity encoder/decoder.

Favicons

In my experience, some RSS readers seem to completely ignore favicons, while some don't, it's a bit inconsistent. Although, after some testing, I found that the most sure way to have an RSS reader actually recognize and fetch your website's favicon is to make sure it's located in the root directory of your website and that it's named something like "favicon.ico".

Linking your RSS feed in the <head> section

After you have created your RSS feed, you can add a button to your site linking to it.

But, in addition to that, you can also add a <link> tag inside your website's <head> tag and link your RSS feed there as well. You can even have multiple feeds, just give them descriptive names that make sense! See the code below...

Linking the RSS feed

Put this inside the <head> tag:

<link 
    rel="alternate" 
    type="application/rss+xml" 
    title="RSS feed for my site" 
    href="https://my-website/rss.xml" 
/>

What it does is it makes it your RSS feed autodiscoverable... Sorta. Well, back in the ye olden days, most browsers had a built-in RSS reader and they could automatically detect if a website had RSS feed. Nowadays, that functionality is mostly gone (shame!), but, fret not, at least for Firefox, there are extensions such as Awesome RSS that returns that functionality! After you install it, if a website has a fetchable RSS feed, at the corner of your browser's address bar you will see an RSS icon.

Final words

Also, one more thing. When troubleshooting your RSS feed in RSS readers, remember to occasionally unsubscribe and resubscribe and clear cache! Because often you just need to refresh it for your changes to show up.

You can read more about the official RSS documentation on RSS Advisory Board.
